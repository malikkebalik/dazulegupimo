Was Willi Ninja Trans Or Gay?

William Roscoe Leake, better known as Willi Ninja, was iconic in the New York City dance and LGBTQ+ scene. While not transgender, Ninja identified as a gay man.

Despite this, he was undeniably influential in the lives of many transgender and gay individuals, serving as the ‘mother’ of the House of Ninja, which he founded in 1982.

Growing up in Flushing, Queens, Ninja’s love for dance began at a young age, a passion his mother, Esther Leake, nurtured by taking him to ballet and performances at the Apollo Theater.

During these formative years, Ninja began to understand his sexual identity. He would later recount how his mother, having recognized his homosexuality, affirmed her unconditional love for him.

This support undoubtedly played a part in shaping the inclusive and accepting figure Ninja would later become.

His early years as a dancer saw him forming the Video Pretenders, which specialized in replicating music video choreography.

Eventually, they realized the potential of creating their unique dance movements, marking the early stages of Ninja’s creation and popularizing voguing. This dance form incorporated elaborate model-like poses and complex choreography.

The broader Harlem drag ball scene allowed Ninja to express his androgynous gender presentation, challenging societal norms on and off the stage.

However, after his appearance in the documentary Paris is Burning, Ninja achieved fame, expanding his roles to include choreographer, musician, runway model, and modeling coach, all while inspiring various artists worldwide.

Willi Ninja Sexuality And Gender

While Willi Ninja identified as a gay man, his contribution to challenging and redefining gender norms cannot be underestimated. Through his involvement in the Harlem drag ball scene, Ninja transgressed gender boundaries.

He did not attempt to pass as a woman, instead opting for a blend of masculinity and femininity in his performance and appearance. This distinctively androgynous persona and his towering talent set him apart.

This disruption of traditional gender norms extended to his influence on voguing. Inspired by Paris DuPree, Ninja revolutionized the dance form, adding swift angular movements and contortionist positions.

His dance style took voguing to unprecedented visibility and perfection, giving it worldwide recognition as a legitimate dance form. The House of Ninja, which Ninja founded, became known for its exceptional dancers.

Ninja cultivated an extended social family and support network within this house for his adopted gay and transgender children. He offered a platform for these young individuals, many of whom had been disowned by their families.

Ninja’s advocacy wasn’t limited to the stage. Offstage, he initiated conversations about HIV/AIDS prevention within the ballroom scene during the 1980s, when this topic was often met with silence.

Ninja was diagnosed with HIV in 2003, but despite his declining health, he continued to support the LGBTQ+ community, mentoring up-and-coming dancers and models until he died in 2006 from AIDS-related heart failure.

Willi Ninja’s legacy extends far beyond his dance contributions. His fearless challenge of rigid gender barriers and tireless advocacy for HIV/AIDS awareness has solidified his place in history as a trailblazing advocate for the LGBTQ+ community.
